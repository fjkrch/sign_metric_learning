\section*{Discussion}
\vspace{-0.5em}

\begin{sloppypar}
All four source languages yield viable cross-lingual transfer.
Thai$\rightarrow$ASL frozen ({\texttt{raw\_angle}}~95.1\%) is the best
overall source for ASL, surpassing Arabic$\rightarrow$ASL (94.1\%) by
+1.0~pp despite Thai having the fewest training samples (2\,023).
Arabic$\rightarrow$LIBRAS frozen ({\texttt{raw\_angle}}~97.1\%) remains
the best for LIBRAS (+2.1~pp over ASL).
LIBRAS$\rightarrow$Arabic (91.7\%) and Thai$\rightarrow$Arabic (90.2\%)
are competitive with ASL$\rightarrow$Arabic (91.3\%).
Arabic$\rightarrow$Thai (54.6\%) edges out ASL$\rightarrow$Thai (53.2\%)
by +1.4~pp.
\end{sloppypar}

Transfer is \emph{asymmetric}: Arabic$\rightarrow$LIBRAS consistently
outperforms LIBRAS$\rightarrow$Arabic by 4--6~pp, while
Thai$\rightarrow$ASL (95.1\%) outperforms ASL$\rightarrow$Thai (53.2\%)
by a massive 42~pp\textemdash the most extreme asymmetry in our matrix.
Thai remains the hardest target across all sources (50--55\% frozen),
reflecting its 42-class vocabulary and limited training data.

The \texttt{raw} representation dominates when transferring \emph{to} ASL
(from LIBRAS and Arabic), whereas \texttt{raw\_angle} is best for
Thai$\rightarrow$ASL and most LIBRAS/Arabic targets.
Notably, Thai is a surprisingly effective \emph{source} despite being
the hardest \emph{target}, suggesting that its large class diversity
(42 classes) provides rich pre-training signal even with limited samples.
